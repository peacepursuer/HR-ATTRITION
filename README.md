Executive Summary

Employee attrition is one of the most important workforce challenges for organizations because it directly impacts productivity, recruitment costs, morale, and long-term growth. This report analyses employee demographics, turnover trends, and wellness indicators using an interactive Power BI dashboard.
The dataset contains 1,470 employees, of which 84% are active and 16% are inactive. The findings reveal that attrition is strongly influenced by factors such as low performance, overtime, poor work-life balance, low satisfaction levels, and limited career growth opportunities.
The purpose of this report is to explain the insights discovered, identify the major causes of attrition, and recommend practical actions that can help management improve employee retention and workforce well-being.

4. Dashboard Analysis & Findings
4.1 Employee Demographics Dashboard
 
This dashboard provides a high-level overview of workforce composition and inactive employee patterns.
Key Findings
Workforce Status
•	Total Employees: 1,470
•	Active Employees: 84%
•	Inactive Employees: 16%
This means roughly 1 in every 6 employees is no longer active, which may indicate a retention challenge.
Gender Distribution of Inactive Employees
•	Male: 150 employees (63.29%)
•	Female: 87 employees (36.71%)
Male employees account for a larger share of inactive staff. This suggests turnover is more common among male employees in this dataset.
Attrition by Age Group
•	Highest inactive count is within the 31–45 age range with 103 employees
This is a critical age group because it often includes experienced and mid-career professionals. Losing employees in this category can create leadership and skill gaps.
Distance from Work
•	Employees living at a close distance had the highest inactive count (144 employees)
This suggests that commute distance alone may not be the main driver of attrition. Other workplace factors are likely more significant.
Marital Status
•	Majority of inactive employees are Single
Single employees may have greater mobility and flexibility to pursue opportunities elsewhere.
Work-Life Balance
•	Only 9% of employees reported a good work-life balance
Poor work-life balance is a strong warning sign and often leads to stress, disengagement, and turnover.

4.2 Turnover Analysis Dashboard 1
 
This dashboard examines attrition based on performance, overtime, travel frequency, and department.
Key Findings
Low Performance and Attrition
•	84% of attrition cases are linked to low-performing employees
This may indicate:
•	Lack of motivation
•	Poor role fit
•	Inadequate training
•	Weak performance management systems
Overtime and Attrition
•	127 out of 237 employees working overtime became inactive
Overtime is a major risk factor. Persistent long working hours can cause burnout, stress, and reduced job satisfaction.
Business Travel Frequency
•	Employees who rarely travel recorded 156 attrition cases
o	Male: 102
o	Female: 54
This may suggest a lack of role variety, growth exposure, or engagement opportunities.
Departmental Attrition
•	Research & Development recorded the highest attrition rate (56%)
Since R&D is often linked to innovation and technical expertise, high turnover in this department can hurt competitiveness and project continuity.

Key Findings
Job Role Attrition

4.4 Employee Wellness Dashboard
 
This dashboard explores how satisfaction, involvement, promotions, and work-life balance relate to attrition.
Key Findings
Environment Satisfaction
•	Highest attrition occurred among employees who were Very Dissatisfied (72 employees)
Workplace environment strongly affects retention. Employees who dislike their environment are more likely to leave.
Job Involvement
•	Low involvement: 71 attrition cases
•	Moderate involvement: 125 attrition cases
Employees who feel disconnected from their work are less likely to stay long-term.
Job Satisfaction
•	Very Dissatisfied: 66 attrition cases
•	Satisfied: 73 attrition cases
Even some satisfied employees left, suggesting that salary, workload, or external offers may also play a role.
Work-Life Balance
•	Excellent category had the lowest rating:
o	27 employees (11.39%)
This confirms that strong work-life balance is not widely experienced across the workforce.
Promotions
•	92 inactive employees had been promoted (13.5%)
•	674 employees were promoted in the last 5 years
Promotion alone does not guarantee retention. Employees also value leadership quality, compensation, recognition, and workplace culture.

5. Root Causes of Employee Attrition
Based on all dashboard insights, the likely causes of attrition include:
1. Burnout from Overtime
Heavy workloads and long hours can reduce morale and increase stress.
2. Low Job Satisfaction
Employees who feel undervalued or dissatisfied are more likely to leave.
3. Weak Work-Life Balance
Insufficient flexibility or personal time can drive resignations.
4. Limited Growth Opportunities
Entry-level employees may leave if they do not see career advancement.
5. Compensation Issues
Roles such as Laboratory Technicians may feel underpaid compared to workload.
6. Poor Employee Engagement
Low involvement and low performance often reflect disengagement.

9. Conclusion
The HR Attrition Dashboard reveals that employee turnover is not caused by one factor alone. It is driven by a combination of workload pressure, dissatisfaction, weak work-life balance, low engagement, and career concerns.
By acting on these insights, the organization can move from reactive HR management to proactive talent retention. Reducing attrition will improve productivity, save costs, strengthen culture, and create a more engaged workforce.
